Decades of research in network science, social computing, and computational linguistics reveal how communities form, break, and recover. Communitas is built on that foundation.

Here's what we know — and what it means for anyone stewarding a community.

## Communities are dynamic, not static

Work on dynamic social networks shows that communities form, split, and merge over time. A snapshot of "who's in what group" is already stale by the time you look at it. Clusters drift. People shift roles. New subgroups emerge around events or conflicts.

**What this means in practice:** You need a community timeline and a change detector — not a frozen org chart. The useful question isn't "what does our community look like?" but "what changed since last month, and why?"

## Bridges hold everything together

Research on bridges in social networks shows that a small number of people connect otherwise separate clusters. These bridge nodes matter disproportionately: they carry information across groups and hold the network together. Lose them, and the community fragments — often silently.

**What this means in practice:** You need bridge maps showing who connects clusters, and broker load warnings for people who are single points of failure. Most communities don't know who their bridges are until they burn out and leave.

## Information doesn't flow evenly

Studies on information diffusion and opinion dynamics show that how information spreads depends on network topology, tie strength, and content — not follower counts. Some people are structurally unreachable. Some messages die in a cluster. Newcomers are often the most isolated.

**What this means in practice:** Pro-social diffusion tools — "help a newcomer find the right thread," "surface this to the people it affects" — are more valuable than viral growth hacks. Reach is a structural property, not a content strategy.

## Language reveals community health

Research on linguistic style matching shows that conversational patterns — how people mirror each other's language, how threads are structured, how conflict escalates — correlate with rapport, retention, and sense of virtual community. Work on algospeak shows that platform incentives reshape how communities talk, often eroding authenticity.

**What this means in practice:** A community's voice is a signal, not a brand asset to manufacture. Facilitation prompts, de-escalation suggestions, and conversational structure analysis support healthier interactions — without replacing the community's own language.

## Moderation is about legitimacy, not accuracy

The field is shifting from moderation-as-accuracy (did the classifier get it right?) to moderation-as-legitimacy (do members trust the process?). Research on participatory governance and policy-as-prompt shows that how decisions are made matters as much as what decisions are made. The evidence is unambiguous: undisclosed AI participation triggers severe backlash and erodes trust.

**What this means in practice:** Augmentation, not replacement. AI drafts; humans decide. Every intervention is logged and explainable. Communities set their own policies; tooling operationalizes them. Longitudinal auditing catches drift before it becomes harm.

## Knowledge graphs ground recommendations in evidence

GraphRAG — combining graph structure with language model prompting — enables a new kind of sensemaking. A research corpus becomes a navigable knowledge graph: papers linked to claims, claims to methods, methods to interventions, interventions to outcomes.

**What this means in practice:** When Communitas suggests an intervention, it cites the evidence and the mechanism — and flags when evidence is weak or context doesn't match. Recommendations become auditable arguments, not opaque scores.

This is the research foundation Communitas builds on — not a single breakthrough, but a convergence of dynamic networks, bridge theory, diffusion models, computational linguistics, governance research, and knowledge graphs, applied to the practical work of helping communities thrive.
